Network communication issues

June 30, 2026 · 9 min read · CAN/LIN Systems

Modern vehicles are networks. Multiple ECUs (engine, transmission, body, gateway) communicate constantly over CAN (Controller Area Network) and LIN (Local Interconnect Network) buses. When communication fails, you get cascade faults: the dash goes dark, the transmission won't shift, or nothing responds to diagnostics. These are some of the hardest faults to diagnose, but the methodology is systematic and trackable.

CAN bus architecture: Understanding the network

CAN overview

  • Purpose: Connects all vehicle modules and allows them to share data
  • Speed: Typically 250 kbps or 500 kbps
  • Physical: Two wires (CAN High = Pin 6, CAN Low = Pin 14 on OBD2 connector)
  • Protocol: Differential signaling—CAN High and CAN Low voltage difference carries the signal

How it works:

  • All modules listen to the same wires simultaneously (shared bus)
  • When module A wants to send data, it transmits; all others receive
  • Collision detection: If two modules transmit simultaneously, lower-priority message yields
  • No central controller; all modules communicate peer-to-peer

LIN buses

  • Purpose: Lower-speed, simpler communications (climate, lighting, seats)
  • Speed: 10 kbps typical (much slower than CAN)
  • Physical: Single wire (shared ground)
  • Master/slave: One module (e.g., Body Control Module) controls LIN slaves
💡 Key difference: CAN is peer-to-peer; LIN is master-controlled. A failed LIN master affects all LIN slaves. A failed CAN node might only affect its own functionalities.

Common network communication faults

Complete CAN bus down ("no communication with vehicle")

Your scan tool can't reach any modules.

  • Likely causes: Open or shorted CAN wires, gateway module failure, battery disconnect, OBD2 connector issue
  • First check: Test OBD2 connector pins 4 (ground) and 16 (power) for voltage
  • Next: Disconnect DLC breakout box; measure CAN High/Low at OBD2 pins 6 and 14

Partial communication (some modules respond, others don't)

  • Likely causes: Failed individual module, specific CAN branch disconnected, gateway module not forwarding data
  • Diagnosis: Scan for modules that respond (engine, body, etc. that DO communicate)
  • Isolate: Offending module likely failed or disconnected from network

Module lockout / gateway refused communication

  • Symptom: Scanner connects but modules report "not responding" or "service required"
  • Cause: Security lockout after too many failed access attempts or factory reset
  • Solution: Gateway module reset (typically 30+ minutes with specific procedures per OEM)

CAN bus diagnostic workflow

Step 1: Verify vehicle power

  • Battery voltage 12.5V minimum
  • OBD2 power pin (16) shows ~12V
  • OBD2 ground pin (4) shows <0.5 ohm to battery negative

Step 2: Probe the OBD2 connector

  • Use multimeter to measure voltage on pins 6 (CAN High) and 14 (CAN Low)
  • Should show ~2.5V each at rest (5V total between them)
  • During bus activity, they oscillate but stay between 0–5V

Normal CAN voltage behavior:

  • At rest: CAN High ≈ 2.5V, CAN Low ≈ 2.5V (balanced)
  • During messaging: CAN High rises to ~4V, CAN Low drops to ~1V (differential signal)
  • No activity: Both wires stuck at 0V or 5V = short circuit
🔧 Pro tip: Use an oscilloscope on CAN bus pins to see actual waveforms. A clean, regular square wave pattern indicates healthy bus activity. Garbled or flat signals indicate corruption or electrical issue.

Step 3: Locate the DLC and check connectors

  • CAN wires typically run from OBD2 DLC to gateway module (usually near firewall or under dash)
  • Visually inspect for corroded, loose, or water-damaged connectors
  • Check for chafed wires (owner may have added aftermarket equipment)

Step 4: Isolate CAN branches

  • On vehicles with multiple CAN networks (body, powertrain, infotainment), determine which is down
  • Use scanner to identify which modules are responding vs. silent
  • Silent modules suggest that CAN branch is disconnected

Step 5: Test with mulit-meter across CAN pins

  • Measure resistance between CAN High and CAN Low at OBD2 DLC (key off):
  • Should read 60 ohms (two 120-ohm terminators in parallel)
  • If <10 ohms or >100 ohms, indicates short circuit or open circuit

Step 6: Disconnect suspect modules one at a time

  • If bus was shorted between a module connector, unplugging it should restore communication
  • Replacement module usually fixes the issue

Common communication-related DTCs

U0100 – Lost Communication with ECU/PCM

  • Cause: ECU offline, CAN wire broken, gateway issue
  • Test: Can you read other modules? If yes, ECU-specific issue. If no, whole bus is down.

U0101 – Lost Communication with Transmission Control Module

  • Same as above but for transmission module
  • Often paired with: P0700 (transmission problem) or transmission limp mode

U0120 – Lost Communication with Body Control Module

  • Affects lighting, door locks, climate, seat controls
  • May show as "all dash lights out" or "won't lock/unlock"

U1000 – CAN Bus Fault

  • Generic CAN problem: Could be any network issue
  • Investigation: Pull freeze frames; what was happening when fault occurred?
💡 Hint: U-codes (U0xxx) are always communication faults. P-codes indicate the module itself is malfunctioning. If you see U-codes alongside P-codes, the module may be failing AND the bus may be struggling.

Repair strategies

CAN wire damage (chafed, broken intern insulation)

  • Solution: Repair or replace wiring; re-crimp connectors
  • Prevention: Verify aftermarket equipment (alarm systems, etc.) aren't chafing factory harness

Corroded OBD2 connector or DLC pins

  • Solution: Clean with electronics cleaner, or replace DLC connector assembly
  • Cost: Usually <$100 in parts; real cost is diagnosis

Failed gateway module

  • Common on vehicles after collision: Gateway unit may have been struck or suffered electrical surge
  • Solution: Replace and reprogram with VIN/configuration data
  • Cost: $800–$2,000+ (module, programming, diagnostics)

Module offline due to power or ground fault

  • Test: Measure power and ground at module connectors
  • Solution: Repair power/ground circuits; may involve BCM relay or fuse

Equipment investment for CAN diagnostics

  • Oscilloscope: Most valuable tool ($2,000–$5,000 handheld scope with async trigger)
  • DLC breakout box: Allows monitoring individual pins without damage ($100–$300)
  • CAN bus analyzer: Specialized (overkill for most shops unless high-volume diagnostics)
