Vehicle network communication issues: CAN bus troubleshooting

June 30, 2026 · 8 min read · Diagnostics & Networks

Modern vehicles are essentially rolling computers, with dozens of modules sharing data over multiple networks. When the CAN bus fails—or parts of it do—you get a cascade of faults. A single wiring issue can disable transmission, disable ABS, kill the instrument cluster, and trigger limp mode. Understanding network architecture and troubleshooting methodology is essential.

Common CAN bus faults and symptoms

  • Multiple unrelated DTCs: Multiple modules report faults despite being unrelated
  • Missing modules: Scan tool can't communicate with specific modules (BCM, TCM, etc.)
  • Limp mode: Vehicle enters reduced power mode due to lost communication
  • Dead gauge cluster: Speedometer, fuel gauge, warning lights go dark
  • Intermittent faults: Faults come and go; worse on bumps or specific roads

Vehicle network architecture

The CAN bus (high-speed, critical)

Connects critical modules like ECM, TCM, ABS, BCM for real-time communication.

  • Speed: 250 kbps or 500 kbps
  • Pins: 6 (CAN High), 14 (CAN Low) on OBD2 connector
  • Topology: Daisy-chain; breaks anywhere and entire network fails
  • Termination: 120Ω resistors at both ends; missing or blown resistor kills bus

LIN bus (low-speed, peripheral)

Connects non-critical modules: seat heaters, window motors, door locks, HVAC blower.

  • Speed: 9.6 kbps (very slow)
  • Pins: Manufacturer-specific (not standardized like CAN)
  • Design: Loss of LIN bus won't prevent driving; comfort features stop working

FlexRay (next-gen, premium vehicles)

Ultra-high speed network used by luxury brands for ADAS and autonomous systems.

  • Speed: 10 Mbps (40× faster than CAN)
  • Use: BMW, Mercedes, newer Audi models
  • Diagnostic challenge: Requires sophisticated scope and OEM tools
💡 Network concept: Think of CAN like a single main highway (all traffic shares one path). If the highway is blocked, nothing moves. LIN is like side roads (loss doesn't affect main travel). FlexRay is a multi-lane expressway with lanes dedicated to different traffic types.

CAN bus diagnostic workflow

Step 1: Verify DLC power and ground

Before touching CAN pins:

  • Pin 16 (battery 12V): Should read ~12.6V with engine off
  • Pins 4 & 5 (ground): Should show 0.0V relative to battery negative
  • If missing: Check main power relay and PCM ground connections

Step 2: Check CAN bus voltage

With scope, monitor CAN High (pin 6) and CAN Low (pin 14):

  • Normal at idle: Both pins should oscillate around 2.5V (midpoint between 0V and 5V)
  • Dormant bus: If both sit at 2.5V flat (no activity), no modules are transmitting
  • One stuck high: 5V on CAN High, near 0V on CAN Low = open wire on high side
  • One stuck low: 0V on CAN High, 5V on CAN Low = short to ground on high side
⚠️ Critical: CAN lines are extremely sensitive to voltage. Even a 1V offset from proper levels indicates a problem. Use a scope, not just a multimeter.

Step 3: Check termination resistors

CAN requires 120Ω resistors at both ends to prevent signal reflection.

  • Location: Usually in ECM and Gateway module (varies by vehicle)
  • Test: Disconnect CAN pins and measure resistance with multimeter
  • Expected: ~60Ω between CAN High and CAN Low (two 120Ω resistors in parallel)
  • If open (∞ Ω): Missing or blown terminator; bus won't work
  • If too low (<50Ω): Shorted resistor or shorted wires

Step 4: Check for shorts and continuity

  • CAN High to CAN Low: Should not be shorted (tests for 120Ω termination resistance, not direct short)
  • CAN High to ground: Should be open (∞ Ω)
  • CAN Low to battery 12V: Should be open
  • CAN pins to chassis/frame: Should be open

Step 5: Monitor CAN traffic during fault

  • Capture CAN waveforms while the fault occurs (or engine running)
  • Look for:
  • Clean square wave pattern at CAN High and CAN Low
  • Consistent period (250 kbps = 4 µs per bit)
  • No noise spikes or glitches
  • Intermittent faults: Capture the exact moment communication drops

Common CAN network faults

Corroded DLC connector

Cause: Water intrusion, corrosion on pins 6 and 14

  • Symptom: Intermittent faults, worse after rain
  • Fix: Clean connector contacts or replace DLC connector (~$50–$200)

Cut or damaged CAN wiring

Cause: Crash damage, steering column collapse, sharp edges in cabin

  • Diagnosis: Check wiring continuity throughout CAN harness
  • Common location: Behind steering column, under driver seat, rear hatch

CAN terminator removed or failed

Cause: Service work removed resistor, resistor failed electrically

  • Impact: Bus is completely non-functional
  • Fix: Verify terminator resistance; replace if needed

Multiple modules failing simultaneously (real)

Cause: Power supply or ground rail failure affecting multiple modules

  • Example: Main PCM relay fails → ECM, TCM, BCM all lose power → massive fault cascade
  • Diagnosis: Check 12V and ground supply to each affected module

Intermittent bus faults (nightmare to diagnose)

  • Cause: Loose connector, faulty terminator, weak contact
  • Technique: Bump and wiggle connectors while watching CAN traffic on scope
  • Solution: Crimp connectors, clean contacts, or replace harness

Tools for CAN diagnostics

  • Digital oscilloscope: Non-negotiable for proper CAN analysis (100 MHz+)
  • OEM scan tool: To read live module list and verify module presence
  • Multimeter with resistance range: Test continuity and termination
  • CAN breakout box: Monitor individual pins safely under the vehicle
  • Network analyzer (advanced): Captures and decodes CAN messages (~$3,000+)

When it's NOT the bus

Sometimes, "CAN bus fault" codes hide real problems:

  • Module crashes: Bad ECM that drops off bus (replace ECM)
  • Power issues: Modules lose 12V; seem to drop off bus (fix charging/battery)
  • Corroded ground stud: Modules get power but bad ground path (clean ground connections)
