Diagnostic Labor Rates: Why Shops Leave Money on the Table

By Mike Miller

The Flat-Rate Trap

Walk into most independent repair shops and ask about diagnostic pricing, and you'll hear something like: "We charge $89.95 for engine diagnostics" or "It's $125 for transmission codes." This flat-rate approach feels clean and easy to quote, but it's quietly destroying shop profitability. Here's why: a transmission fault that takes 45 minutes to diagnose on a 2024 GMC earns the same $125 as a transmission fault that takes 2.5 hours to diagnose on a 2015 Duramax with module access issues. In the first case, the tech earned $167 per hour. In the second, they earned $50 per hour. The shop ate the difference. When you multiply this across dozens of diagnostic jobs per month, the revenue leak becomes massive.

Diagnostics Is High-Skill Work — Price It That Way

Modern vehicle diagnostics require expensive equipment, up-to-date software subscriptions, technical knowledge that takes years to develop, and the right mindset to solve problems under time pressure. A competent diagnostic technician is one of your most valuable assets, yet many shops price their time at or below general repair labor rates. This is backward. Diagnostics should command a premium — typically 15 to 25 percent above base shop rate — because the stakes are higher, the skill ceiling is higher, and the consequences of poor work are more expensive than misdiagnosis of a transmission rebuild. When you undercharge for this work, you're essentially subsidizing customer indecision and leaving money on the table that should fund your equipment upgrades and training.

The Real Cost of Equipment and Software

A professional TEXA diagnostic platform like the IDC5 or Axone Voice costs $8,000 to $15,000 upfront. Annual software subscriptions run $2,000 to $4,000. Vehicle coverage databases, OEM integration, and real-time data require ongoing investment. That equipment needs to generate revenue, and it does — but only if diagnostic labor is priced to support the investment. A shop charging $89.95 flat-rate for diagnostics will need to perform 80 to 100 complete diagnostic jobs just to cover the annual software subscription. Most shops aren't doing that, which means the tool investment is already underwater before it generates profit. Proper pricing of $150 to $175 per diagnostic labor hour makes the math work and justifies the equipment investment to your finance team.

Time-and-Material Diagnostics Work Better

The most profitable and transparent approach is time-and-material diagnostic labor. Charge your standard billable labor rate (typically $95 to $140 per hour in most markets) for diagnostic time, and apply it to however long the diagnosis takes. A 30-minute diagnosis costs one customer $50 to $70. A 2.5-hour diagnosis costs another customer $240 to $350. Both are priced fairly based on actual work performed. Customers appreciate the transparency of "here's how long it took, here's what we found," and they understand that some vehicles are more complex than others. You capture the full value of your technician's time and your equipment's capability. No more guessing whether you priced the job correctly or left money on the table.

The Diagnostic Fee Justification

Many shops worry that time-and-material diagnostics will result in customer sticker shock or lost jobs. In practice, the opposite happens when you communicate the value clearly. An upfront conversation — "We'll diagnose your vehicle and charge you for the time it takes, typically $75 to $150 depending on complexity. If you move forward with repairs, we'll apply a portion of the diagnostic fee toward your repair bill" — sets expectations and builds trust. Customers see the integrity in this approach. They also see that a diagnostic that costs $125 in time saved them from a $1,200 parts-and-labor guessing game. That's a win for everyone, and it positions your shop as competent and fair, not greedy or exploratory.

Reclaim Your Diagnostic Revenue

If your shop is currently using flat-rate diagnostic pricing, you're almost certainly leaving 20 to 40 percent of potential revenue on the table every month. Switching to time-and-material pricing for diagnostics — with your professional scan tool recording the actual time spent — aligns your compensation with the actual work performed and with the value your equipment and expertise deliver. This isn't about squeezing customers; it's about compensating your people fairly and justifying the investment in professional diagnostic capability.
