Diagnosing Intermittent Electrical Problems: The Faults That Drive Shops Crazy

By Mike Miller

The Most Frustrating Jobs in the Shop

Every technician knows the scenario. A customer brings in their vehicle with a complaint that happens "sometimes" — a dash light that flickers occasionally, a power window that works most of the time, an engine that stumbles once a week. The vehicle arrives at your bay, and it works perfectly. No codes stored. Nothing visibly wrong. The customer insists the problem is real, and you believe them, but finding a fault that won't show itself is one of the hardest things we do. Intermittent electrical problems account for some of the highest comeback rates in our industry because they refuse to cooperate with traditional diagnostic approaches.

Why Traditional Methods Fail

The standard diagnostic workflow breaks down with intermittents. Hook up a scan tool, read codes, verify with live data, make the repair. Simple. But when the fault only happens under specific conditions — after the vehicle warms up, when it's raining, during hard acceleration, or at random intervals — you can't just capture it on demand. Budget scan tools make this worse because they only capture data when you're actively connected and watching. The fault happens when the vehicle is driving home, not when it's parked in your shop with a tech staring at a screen.

Data Logging Changes the Game

Professional diagnostic platforms with continuous data logging capability solve a problem that no amount of testing skill can overcome: you can't be watching every circuit all the time. Data logging lets you record relevant parameters over extended periods — hours or even days — and review the information after the fact. When the customer reports the fault happened Tuesday at 3 PM during their commute, you can pull the logged data and see exactly what the system was doing at that moment. Voltage drops, signal interruptions, communication glitches — they all leave traces in the data if you're recording it.

Capturing the Conditions That Cause the Fault

Intermittent problems typically have triggers, even when the customer can't identify them. Temperature is a common factor — thermal expansion affects connectors and solder joints. Vibration is another — a slightly backed-out terminal might maintain contact until the vehicle hits a pothole. Humidity and moisture affect ground circuits and corroded connections. Professional diagnostic tools let you correlate fault events with environmental conditions by logging not just the symptom data but also coolant temperature, ambient temperature, vehicle speed, and engine load. When you see that every fault event happens above 180°F coolant temperature, you've narrowed your search to components affected by heat soak.

Module Communication Monitoring

Modern vehicles have 30 to 100 networked modules communicating over CAN bus, LIN bus, and other protocols. An intermittent network fault can cause symptoms that seem unrelated to the actual problem location. A body control module with a flaky connection might cause the instrument cluster to drop out momentarily, the radio to reset, and the power locks to stop responding — three unrelated complaints with a single root cause. Professional scan tools can monitor network traffic and flag communication errors, timing anomalies, and message dropouts that point to the failing module or harness section. Without this capability, you're chasing symptoms instead of causes.

Connector and Harness Testing

A large percentage of intermittent electrical problems trace back to connector issues — terminal back-out, corrosion, fretting, or heat damage. The challenge is that these problems often don't show during static testing. A connector might have good continuity when measured with a meter but fail under vibration or load. Professional diagnostic routines can stress-test circuits by commanding components while monitoring for voltage drops and signal integrity. Some advanced platforms support terminal tension testing protocols and connector mating force verification. These aren't theoretical exercises — they're practical methods for finding problems that a multimeter alone will miss.

Building a Systematic Approach

Successful intermittent diagnosis requires a different mindset than regular troubleshooting. First, document the customer complaint in detail: when does it happen, how often, under what conditions, and what exactly does the symptom look like? Second, identify the systems and circuits involved and set up logging to capture relevant data. Third, duplicate the driving conditions that reportedly trigger the fault — this might mean test driving under specific circumstances or having the customer keep the vehicle for extended logging. Fourth, review the captured data for anomalies. Fifth, isolate and verify the root cause before making repairs. This takes longer than a straightforward diagnosis, but it dramatically reduces comebacks.
